Common questions

Zinc — your questions answered

The most common questions about zinc supplementation, with links to full articles on each subtopic.

Zinc deficiency is often subtle. The most common signs include frequent infections or slow recovery from illness, hair thinning, delayed wound healing, white spots on nails, dry skin, and reduced sense of taste or smell. A blood test can confirm deficiency.
📌 Read full article: "Signs of zinc deficiency — how to recognise it" →
Zinc bisglycinate has high bioavailability and is gentle on the stomach. Zinc citrate is also well absorbed. Zinc oxide and zinc sulphate are common in cheaper supplements but have lower absorption rates.
📌 Read full article: "Zinc bisglycinate vs other forms — which is best?" →
The EU reference intake is 10 mg per day for adults. The EFSA tolerable upper intake level is 25 mg per day — exceeding this consistently can interfere with copper absorption.
📌 Read full article: "How much zinc do you need per day?" →
Take zinc with a small meal to avoid nausea. Avoid taking it alongside high-phytate foods or calcium supplements. Space zinc and iron supplements apart.
📌 Read full article: "When and how to take zinc supplements" →
Zinc has anti-inflammatory properties and inhibits Cutibacterium acnes. Clinical trials show modest benefits for mild-to-moderate acne. It is not as effective as prescription treatments for severe cases.
📌 Read full article: "Does zinc help with acne? What science says" →
Higher-risk groups include vegetarians and vegans, older adults, pregnant and breastfeeding women, people with digestive conditions, and athletes with high sweat losses.
📌 Read full article: "Who is at risk of zinc deficiency?" →
Best dietary sources: oysters, red meat, poultry and shellfish. Plant sources (pumpkin seeds, legumes, whole grains) contain phytates that reduce absorption. A plant-based diet may benefit from supplementation.
📌 Read full article: "Best dietary sources of zinc" →
