Zinc: what it does, who needs it and how to supplement it

Zinc is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in the body. It supports immunity, skin health, hormonal balance and wound healing — yet deficiency is common and often unrecognised.

300+ enzymatic reactions
17% of the global population is deficient

What zinc does

Key functions of zinc in the body

Zinc is an essential trace mineral — your body cannot produce or store it, so regular intake through diet or supplementation is necessary.

Immune system support

Zinc is essential for the development and function of immune cells. Even mild deficiency impairs immune response and increases susceptibility to infection.

Skin, hair and nails

Zinc supports collagen synthesis and cell renewal. It is one of the most studied minerals in the context of acne, wound healing and hair loss.

Hormonal balance

Zinc plays a role in testosterone production and thyroid hormone metabolism. It is particularly relevant for men's health and reproductive function.

Antioxidant defence

Zinc is a component of superoxide dismutase (SOD), one of the body's primary antioxidant enzymes. It helps protect cells from oxidative stress.

Cognitive function

Zinc modulates neurotransmitter activity and is concentrated in areas of the brain involved in learning and memory. Deficiency has been linked to cognitive decline.

Fertility and pregnancy

Zinc is critical for DNA synthesis, cell division and fetal development. Both deficiency and excess can affect fertility in men and women.

Common questions

Zinc — your questions answered

The most common questions about zinc supplementation, answered based on current scientific evidence.

Zinc deficiency is often subtle. The most common signs include frequent infections or slow recovery from illness, hair thinning or loss, delayed wound healing, white spots on nails, dry skin, reduced sense of taste or smell, and — in more severe cases — impaired growth in children. A blood test can confirm deficiency, though serum zinc levels don't always reflect tissue zinc status accurately.
Not all zinc forms are absorbed equally. Zinc bisglycinate (zinc bound to the amino acid glycine) has high bioavailability and is gentle on the stomach — making it a good choice for daily use. Zinc citrate is also well absorbed. Zinc oxide and zinc sulphate are common in cheaper supplements but have lower absorption rates. Avoid supplements that don't specify the form.
The EU reference intake is 10 mg per day for adults. Most supplements contain 10–25 mg. The EFSA tolerable upper intake level is 25 mg per day — exceeding this consistently can interfere with copper absorption. If supplementing for a specific reason (e.g. acne, immune support), 15–25 mg daily is common, but long-term high doses should be discussed with a healthcare professional.
Zinc can cause nausea when taken on an empty stomach — taking it with a small meal reduces this. Avoid taking zinc alongside high-phytate foods (whole grains, legumes) or calcium supplements, as these can reduce absorption. Zinc and iron supplements also compete for absorption — space them apart if taking both.
Groups with higher risk include vegetarians and vegans (plant zinc is less bioavailable), older adults, pregnant and breastfeeding women, people with digestive conditions that affect absorption (Crohn's disease, celiac disease), and athletes with high sweat losses. Children with poor dietary variety are also at risk.
The best dietary sources of zinc are oysters, red meat, poultry and shellfish. Plant-based sources include pumpkin seeds, legumes, whole grains and nuts — but these contain phytates that reduce zinc absorption. A varied diet including animal protein usually provides adequate zinc; those following a plant-based diet may benefit from supplementation or soaking/fermenting legumes to reduce phytate content.
Zinc has anti-inflammatory properties and inhibits the growth of Cutibacterium acnes, the bacteria associated with acne. Several clinical trials have shown modest benefits for mild-to-moderate acne. Zinc is not as effective as prescription treatments for severe acne, but it is a well-tolerated option for mild cases and for those who cannot use antibiotics. Zinc bisglycinate or zinc citrate at 25–30 mg daily are the forms most studied in this context.
